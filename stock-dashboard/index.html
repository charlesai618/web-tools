What is RSI? The Relative Strength Index measures momentum on a 0–100 scale. Below 30 = oversold (sellers may be exhausted), above 70 = overbought (buyers may be exhausted). The middle zone (30–70) is neutral territory.

What is MACD? Compares a fast (12-day) and slow (26-day) moving average to detect momentum shifts. When the MACD line crosses above the signal line = bullish momentum building. When it crosses below = bearish momentum. The histogram shows the gap between them.

What is ADX? The Average Directional Index measures trend strength — not direction. Below 20 = no real trend (choppy), 25–50 = strong trend, 50+ = very strong trend. The +DI vs −DI lines reveal whether that trend is up or down.

What are MAs? A moving average smooths out price noise. When price is above an MA, that timeframe is bullish. Below it = bearish. Short MAs (5, 10) reflect recent momentum; long MAs (100, 200) reflect the bigger trend. A Golden Cross (MA50 crosses above MA200) is a strong bullish signal; a Death Cross (MA50 below MA200) is bearish.

Put/Call Ratio: >1.0 = more puts than calls = traders are hedging or betting on a drop. <0.7 = more calls = bullish sentiment. Extreme readings can be contrarian signals.
Implied Volatility: How much the market expects the price to move. High IV = expensive options, fearful market. Low IV = calm market.
Max Pain: The price at which the most options expire worthless — market makers are incentivised to pin price here near expiry.

What is Fibonacci Retracement? Key price levels derived from the Fibonacci sequence. Traders watch these as natural support and resistance zones. 38.2%, 50%, and 61.8% are the most significant levels. A bounce off the 61.8% golden ratio level is one of the most reliable patterns in technical analysis. Extensions (127.2%, 161.8%) project potential targets beyond the swing range.

What is a 1-Day ATM Straddle? Buying both an ATM call and put captures the next day's move. The theoretical price is computed via Black-Scholes using 30-day historical volatility with T=1 trading day. Realized move is the actual next-day absolute price change. When theoretical > realized: options were overpriced — sellers won. When realized > theoretical: the actual move beat what options priced in — buyers won.
